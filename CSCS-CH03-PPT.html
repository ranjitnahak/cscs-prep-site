Topic A — Foundation

Bioenergetics

Bioenergetics
Flow of energy in a biological system — conversion of macronutrients (carbohydrate, protein, fats) into usable energy to perform biological work.

Bioenergetics is the study of how energy flows through living systems — the foundation for every training and nutrition decision in S&C coaching.

Topic A — Foundation

Catabolism & Anabolism

Catabolism
Breakdown of large molecules → smaller molecules with release of energy.

Example: protein → amino acids
Anabolism
Synthesis of larger molecules from smaller ones using energy from catabolic reactions.

Example: amino acids → proteins

Catabolism releases energy. Anabolism uses that energy to build. ATP couples the two — energy from catabolism powers anabolic processes.

Topic A — Foundation

Exergonic & Endergonic Reactions

Exergonic
Energy-releasing reactions. Generally catabolic.

Coupled to endergonic reactions via ATP.

Example: ATP hydrolysis releases energy → drives muscle contraction.
Endergonic
Energy-requiring reactions. Anabolic processes and muscle contraction.

Cannot proceed without a coupled exergonic reaction supplying energy via ATP.

Example: muscle contraction, protein synthesis.

ATP is the bridge — it accepts energy from exergonic (catabolic) reactions and delivers it to endergonic (anabolic + mechanical) processes.

Topic A — Foundation

Metabolism

Metabolism
The sum of all catabolic/exergonic and anabolic/endergonic reactions in a biological system.

• Catabolism = breakdown → energy release (exergonic)
• Anabolism = building → energy use (endergonic)
• ATP = the intermediary molecule coupling them
Fatigue = metabolism failing to meet demand. When ATP supply doesn't meet ATP demand, performance degrades. Understanding metabolism lets us design training that raises the ceiling.
Fig — ATP structure
Topic A — ATP

Adenosine Triphosphate

Orange bonds = high-energy bonds. Breaking the γP–βP bond releases energy — powers muscle contraction, Ca²⁺ pumping, and Na⁺-K⁺ gradient maintenance.
Muscle cells store very little ATP — 80–100 g (≈3 oz) total. Must be constantly resynthesised during exercise.

ATP is the universal energy currency — directly powers all endergonic biological work. Food energy must first be converted to ATP.

Topic A — ATPase Enzymes

ATPase Enzymes

Myosin ATPase
Catalyzes ATP hydrolysis for crossbridge recycling during muscle contraction.
Calcium ATPase
Pumps Ca²⁺ back into the sarcoplasmic reticulum following muscle contraction.
Na⁺-K⁺ ATPase
Maintains the sarcolemmal concentration gradient after membrane depolarization.

Each ATPase serves a specific site — myosin (crossbridge), calcium (SR pump), Na⁺-K⁺ (membrane gradient). All three require continuous ATP to sustain muscle function.

Topic A — Key Concept

Steady State vs Fatigue

Steady State
→ ATP supply equals demand
→ Exercise continues without fatigue
→ Metabolic system meets current activity requirement
e.g. Comfortable aerobic run
Fatigue
→ ATP supply does not meet demand
→ Activity hasn't changed — only metabolic capacity has
→ System fails to sustain ATP supply for the demand
e.g. Max sprint late in game — phosphagen depleted

The activity doesn't change — only the metabolic system's capacity to supply ATP changes with fatigue. Training raises that capacity.

Topic A — Key Concept

ATP Depletion & Training Effect

ATP at maximal fatigue
50–60%
Only decreases to 50–60% of pre-exercise levels — never fully depleted. Cellular function requires a minimum.
Training effect
↑ Capacity
Conditioning improves the metabolic system's capacity to supply ATP — same activity, fatigue delayed or avoided.

Training doesn't change the ATP demand — it improves the body's capacity to supply ATP for that demand.

Topic B — Key Distinctions

Aerobic vs Anaerobic — What Changes

Anaerobic
Does not require oxygen to produce ATP
Phosphagen and glycolytic systems — both occur in the sarcoplasm
Only carbohydrate can be metabolized for energy without direct oxygen involvement
Dominates during high-intensity, short-duration exercise (sprints, heavy resistance training)
Aerobic
Requires oxygen as the terminal electron acceptor
Krebs cycle and electron transport chain occur in the mitochondria
Can oxidize carbohydrate, fat, and protein — all three macronutrients
Dominates during low-intensity, long-duration exercise (marathon, cycling)
Exam fact: Only carbohydrate can be metabolised without oxygen. Fat and protein require aerobic pathways.

Phosphagen + glycolytic = anaerobic, sarcoplasm. Krebs cycle + ETC = aerobic, mitochondria.

Section C — Control

Control of the Phosphagen System

Law of Mass Action
Eqs 3.1–3.3 follow the law of mass action — reactant/product concentrations drive reaction direction.

ATP hydrolysis → ADP + Pᵢ rise → creatine kinase and adenylate kinase accelerate.
Near-Equilibrium Reactions
Eqs 3.1–3.3 are near-equilibrium reactions — direction dictated by reactant concentrations. When ATP rises (exercise stops or intensity drops), reactions slow or reverse.

Free creatine is rephosphorylated to CP via aerobic metabolism during recovery.
When glycolysis or oxidative system can supply ATP, creatine kinase slows — ATP stabilises.
Continues until exercise stops or intensity drops enough to spare CP.

Self-regulating: rising ADP drives resynthesis; rising ATP slows/reverses. Concentration gradients control rate — no complex enzyme activation.

Glycolysis — What It Is

What it is
Breakdown of carbohydrate (muscle glycogen or blood glucose) to resynthesize ATP via multiple enzymatic steps. Occurs in the sarcoplasm.
Rate vs Phosphagen
Rate is slower than phosphagen, but capacity is much higher — glycogen and glucose supply is far greater than CP.
End product = pyruvate. Fate depends on energy demand and O₂ availability.

Section D — Metabolic Acidosis

What Causes H⁺ Accumulation

The real source of H⁺
Lactate is not the cause. ATP hydrolysis (Eq. 3.1) outside the mitochondria is the dominant H⁺ source.

Lactate dehydrogenase actually consumes protons — alkalising the cell. Lactic acidosis is a misnomer; metabolic acidosis is the correct term.
Other fatigue factors: Elevated interstitial K⁺ and Pᵢ impairing Ca²⁺ release from the SR — metabolic acidosis alone doesn't explain all peripheral fatigue.

ATP hydrolysis is the primary H⁺ source — not lactate. Lactate dehydrogenase actually consumes protons. Call it metabolic acidosis, not lactic acidosis.

Section D — Metabolic Acidosis

What H⁺ Accumulation Does

Effects of H⁺ accumulation during fatigue
• ↓ intracellular pH
• Inhibits glycolytic enzymes
• Disrupts excitation-contraction coupling
• Inhibits Ca²⁺ binding to troponin
• Disrupts crossbridge recycling

Metabolic acidosis is responsible for much of peripheral fatigue — primarily by disrupting calcium handling and crossbridge kinetics, not just reducing pH.

Section D — Lactate

Lactate — Energy Substrate

Lactate is not a waste product
Lactate is an energy substrate, especially in type I and cardiac muscle fibres.

It can be:
• Oxidised directly in the fibre where it was produced
• Transported in blood to other muscle fibres for oxidation
• Transported to the liver and converted back to glucose
Clearance timeline: Returns to pre-exercise within ~1 hour. Peak ~5 min post-exercise (buffering/transport delay). Active recovery clears lactate faster than passive.

Lactate is a fuel, not a toxin. Its clearance rate is an indirect marker of recovery — not waste removal.

Section D — Lactate

The Cori Cycle

Lactate → Liver → Glucose → Muscle
Lactate produced in muscle → enters blood → transported to the liver → converted back to glucose via gluconeogenesis → released back into blood → used by muscles.

This process — the Cori cycle — recycles lactate as a glucose precursor and is especially important during prolonged exercise and recovery.
Training adaptation: Both aerobic and anaerobic athletes clear lactate faster than untrained. Resistance training produces lactate adaptations similar to endurance training.

The Cori cycle converts lactate into glucose — making lactate an indirect glucose source. Lactate clearance = indirect recovery marker from metabolic acidosis.

Section D — Energy Yield

Substrate-Level Phosphorylation

Two ATP-generating reactions in glycolysis
• Phosphoglycerate kinase (Eq. 3.6): 1,3-bisphosphoglycerate → 3-phosphoglycerate + ATP

• Pyruvate kinase (Eq. 3.7): Phosphoenolpyruvate → Pyruvate + ATP

Gross yield = 4 ATP. Net varies depending on glucose source (2 or 3 ATP).

Substrate-level phosphorylation = ATP made directly in the metabolic pathway, without the ETC. Fast, but limited yield — only 4 ATP gross from glycolysis.

Oxidative Phosphorylation

ATP production via the ETC
ATP production during the ETC is termed oxidative phosphorylation.

It accounts for >90% of all ATP synthesis in aerobic conditions — far exceeding the contribution of substrate-level phosphorylation in glycolysis and the Krebs cycle combined.
Substrate-level vs oxidative phosphorylation: Substrate-level (glycolysis + Krebs) provides ~7 ATP total. Oxidative phosphorylation (ETC) provides ~25 ATP — this is why the oxidative system has the greatest ATP capacity.

Oxidative phosphorylation = >90% of aerobic ATP. The ETC is where the NADH and FADH₂ collected across all three stages are finally converted into usable ATP.

Section E — Protein Oxidation

Protein Oxidation — Pathways

When protein contributes
Protein is not significant for most activities. Increases to 3–18% during prolonged exercise and long-term starvation.
How protein is oxidised
Proteins → amino acids → glucose (gluconeogenesis), pyruvate, or Krebs intermediates.

Primary: BCAAs — leucine, isoleucine, valine. Also: alanine, aspartate, glutamate.
Exam: BCAAs — leucine, isoleucine, valine — are the major amino acids oxidised in skeletal muscle. Ammonia from deamination = fatigue factor.
Section E — Protein Oxidation

Protein Oxidation — Metabolic Consequences

Nitrogenous waste
Degradation → nitrogenous waste via urea formation and ammonia.

Ammonia is toxic and associated with fatigue — particularly relevant during high-volume training.
Gluconeogenesis link
Most amino acids → glucose via gluconeogenesis — links protein metabolism directly to glucose availability during prolonged exercise and recovery.

Protein oxidation produces ammonia (toxic, fatigue-associated) and feeds gluconeogenesis — both consequences matter for high-volume training and prolonged performance.

Section E — Control

Control of the Oxidative System

Rate-Limiting Step
Slowest reaction: isocitrate → α-ketoglutarate, catalysed by isocitrate dehydrogenase.
Stimulated by ADP, inhibited by ATP.
NAD⁺ / FAD²⁺ Availability
Insufficient NAD⁺/FAD²⁺ → Krebs cycle slows. ETC must run fast enough to regenerate carriers — ETC and Krebs are directly coupled.
ETC Regulation
ETC: inhibited by ATP, stimulated by ADP.
GTP accumulation inhibits succinyl-CoA → succinate, slowing the initial Krebs reaction (oxaloacetate + acetyl-CoA → citrate).
If NADH/FADH₂ carriers are saturated, the Krebs cycle slows.
The oxidative system is demand-driven: ADP rises → all three stages accelerate; ATP rises → all inhibit.

Isocitrate dehydrogenase = rate-limiting · ADP stimulates, ATP inhibits Krebs + ETC · NAD⁺/FAD²⁺ availability couples ETC to Krebs rate.

Section H — O₂ Uptake

Oxygen Deficit

What is the oxygen deficit?
At the start of exercise, the aerobic system responds slowly to the initial increase in energy demand.

The anaerobic contribution during this lag period is termed the oxygen deficit.

Anaerobic mechanisms bridge the gap until aerobic metabolism reaches steady state.

The oxygen deficit is the anaerobic cost of starting exercise — the gap between what aerobic metabolism can supply and what the body needs in the first minutes of effort.

Section H — O₂ Uptake

EPOC — Excess Postexercise O₂

What is EPOC?
After exercise, VO₂ remains elevated above resting levels — the EPOC (excess postexercise oxygen consumption).

EPOC ≠ oxygen deficit. Only a small to moderate relationship exists between them.
EPOC factors: O₂ replenishment · ATP/CP resynthesis · ↑ temperature, circulation, ventilation · ↑ triglyceride–fatty acid cycling · ↑ protein turnover · changes in energy efficiency

Oxygen deficit = anaerobic contribution at onset. EPOC = elevated VO₂ after exercise. Related but not equal — EPOC is driven by multiple recovery processes.

Section H — EPOC

Aerobic Exercise & EPOC
Intensity = greatest effect on EPOC
Greatest EPOC: intensity >50–60% VO₂max AND duration >40 min
Brief supramaximal bouts (>100% VO₂max) may induce the greatest EPOC with lower total work

Heavy resistance (3 sets, 8 exercises, 80–90% 1RM) produces greater EPOC than circuit training (4 sets, 15 reps, 50% 1RM)
EPOC is also intensity-dependent for resistance training

EPOC is intensity-dependent for both aerobic and resistance exercise. Supramaximal bouts (>100% VO₂max) and heavy RT produce the greatest EPOC — the body continues elevated metabolism well after the session ends.

Work-to-Rest Ratios — Why & Application

Why long rest for phosphagen?
Full CP resynthesis = 3–8 min despite 30 s half-life. Maximal short efforts need the longest W:R ratios (1:12–1:20) — aerobic mechanisms must replete phosphagen.
Metabolic specificity principle
Correct intensities and rest intervals "select" specific energy systems — more reflective of sport demands, more efficient training.
Team sports: Basketball, football, hockey, kabaddi — HI efforts interspersed with rest. Training should match this profile, not prolonged aerobic work.

Section H — HIIT

HIIT vs Long Slow Distance

Gibala et al. — The Evidence
Equivalent improvements in muscle buffering capacity and glycogen content:

• HIIT: 250% VO₂peak — 4–6 × 30 s sprints
• LSD: 90–120 min cycling at 65% VO₂peak

Both over 6 sessions. Comparable time-trial improvement.

Same results. Far less time.

HIIT provides equivalent physiological adaptations to long slow endurance training in a fraction of the time — making it the more time-efficient choice for athletes with mixed training demands.

Section H — Training Types

Combination, Cross & Concurrent Training

Type 01
Combination Training
Mix of different exercise types for overall fitness — no specific structure implied. Example: 800m runner adding long slow runs alongside event-specific work.
Type 02
Cross-Training
Different modalities sharing the same metabolic adaptation goal without overstressing primary muscles. Example: runner adding rowing to build aerobic capacity.
Type 03
Concurrent Training
Simultaneous anaerobic resistance + aerobic endurance — same session or day. Risk of interference effect on high-strength, high-power capabilities.

These three differ in structure, metabolic intent, and interference risk. Combination = general fitness mix. Cross-training = same goal, different mode. Concurrent = same time, highest interference risk.

Section H — Concurrent Training

The Interference Effect

How concurrent training reduces anaerobic capabilities
Concurrent training may reduce anaerobic capabilities via:

• ↓ rapid voluntary activation
• Chronically low glycogen → limits intracellular signalling during resistance training
• Fibre type transition toward slow-twitch
When aerobic training helps
Aerobic metabolism supports post-exercise VO₂, lactate removal, and PCr restoration. Anaerobic training itself can improve aerobic power and recovery markers — extensive endurance training is not always necessary.
Section H — Concurrent Training

Concurrent Training — Practical Guidance

Guidance: Extensive endurance training may be counterproductive for strength/power athletes. Use metabolically specific interval training instead of long slow distance.

The interference effect is real but not absolute. Anaerobic training itself improves recovery metrics — targeted interval training can cover aerobic needs without compromising strength and power adaptations.
