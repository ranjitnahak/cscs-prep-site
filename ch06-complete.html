Topic 01 — CV Adaptations · 1 of 5

↑ Maximal Cardiac Output

The Most Significant Change
The most significant CV change with 6–12 months of aerobic endurance training is ↑ in maximal cardiac output.
Primary driver ↑ Stroke volume — both LV chamber volume (more blood) and ↑ contractility (stronger ejection).
Maximal HR Generally remains unaltered or may slightly decrease with prolonged training — possibly due to ↑ parasympathetic tone.
LV Remodelling
Aerobic training → volume overload pattern (contrast with RT pressure overload):
↑ LV chamber volume (end-diastolic volume) — more blood per beat
↑ LV wall thickness (modest) — to accommodate larger chamber
↑ LV contractility — stronger, more forceful ejection at rest and during exercise
Result Resting SV rises from ≈79 mL → ≈76 mL (minor) in untrained; elite male athletes: ≈147 mL resting SV.
Aerobic training → ↑ LV chamber volume + contractility → ↑ Stroke volume → ↑ Max cardiac output → ↑ VO₂max
Topic 01 — CV Adaptations · 2 of 5

Resting Bradycardia & HR Response

Resting Bradycardia
Aerobic endurance training ↓ resting HR — a hallmark adaptation known as bradycardia.
SA node discharge Normal discharge rate: 60–80 bpm. Aerobic training → significantly slower discharge due to ↑ parasympathetic tone.
↑ Stroke volume effect More blood per contraction → heart contracts less frequently to achieve the same cardiac output → ↓ resting HR.
Elite aerobic endurance athletes Resting HR commonly 40–60 bpm. Untrained: 76.4 bpm → after training: 57.0 bpm (Table 6.2 data).
HR During Exercise
Submaximal exercise: Significantly lower HR at any given absolute workload — hallmark of aerobic fitness.
HR increases more slowly in trained athletes than in sedentary people for a given workload.
Maximal exercise HR No change or slight decrease with prolonged training.
HR recovery Aerobic endurance-trained athletes show accelerated HR recovery after exercise — important for training monitoring and athlete readiness assessment.
Key Table 6.2 data: Untrained resting HR: 76.4 → 57.0 bpm after training. Elite male: 45 bpm · Elite female: 51 bpm. Maximal HR: 192.8 → 190.8 (minimal change). Lower submaximal HR for the same work = direct measure of improved cardiorespiratory fitness.
Topic 01 — CV Adaptations · 3 of 5

↑ Capillary Density & Blood Volume

Capillary Density
↑ Muscle fiber capillary density observed in response to aerobic endurance training — function of both training volume and intensity.
What capillaries do Deliver O₂, nutrients, hormones. Remove heat + metabolic by-products (CO₂, lactate, H⁺). ↑ density = ↓ diffusion distance = faster exchange.
Table 6.2 data Capillary number/mm²: 289 → 356 (untrained to trained). Number/fiber: 1.39 → 1.95. Elite: 2.15 capillaries/fiber.
Consequence ↑ Capillarisation + ↑ mitochondrial density → improved a-vO₂ difference → ↑ VO₂max.
Blood Volume
Aerobic training → ↑ blood (plasma) volume — supports ↑ SV + cardiac output.
Plasma volume ↑ ↑ Plasma volume (fluid portion) → ↑ total blood volume → supports higher SV at maximal exercise.
Hemoglobin mass ↑ ↑ Total hemoglobin mass (even though hemoglobin %) — the hematocrit % may slightly decrease due to the relatively greater increase in plasma volume (dilutional effect).
Net effect More total O₂-carrying capacity + lower blood viscosity → ↑ cardiac output efficiency.
a-vO₂ difference: VO₂max = max cardiac output × arteriovenous oxygen difference. Aerobic training ↑ both — more O₂ delivered (↑ CO) and more O₂ extracted per unit of blood (↑ a-vO₂ diff) — hence the multiplicative effect on VO₂max.
Topic 01 — CV Adaptations · 4 of 5

VO₂max — Criterion Index of CV Fitness

What VO₂max Is
The most significant measurable adaptation to aerobic endurance training. The criterion index of cardiovascular fitness.
Formula VO₂max = Max cardiac output × Arteriovenous O₂ difference
Determined by Genetics + training program. As intensity ↑, O₂ consumption rises to maximal levels. When O₂ consumption can no longer increase → VO₂max achieved even with continued exercise.
Table 6.2 data Untrained: 36.0 → 48.0 mL·kg⁻¹·min⁻¹. Elite male: 90.6 · Elite female: 72.8 mL·kg⁻¹·min⁻¹.
Training Response
Aerobic training can improve VO₂max by 5–30% depending on starting fitness level and genetic potential.
Elite athletes May show only 5–10% change — already near genetic ceiling. Further performance gains come from ↑ exercise economy + ↑ lactate threshold.
Untrained individuals May ↑ VO₂max by up to 20% (some studies: up to 30%). Most adaptations achievable within 6–12 months.
After plateau Further performance improvements rely on ↑ exercise economy and ↑ lactate threshold rather than additional VO₂max gains.
Interval training: Short, high-intensity bouts of interval sprints can improve maximal oxygen uptake if the interim rest period is also short. A repeated sprint-sequence interval session = ≥2 bouts of ≥10 seconds with recovery ≤60 seconds. Longer rest periods improve sprint speed but not maximal aerobic power.
Topic 01 — CV Adaptations · 5 of 5

Lactate Threshold & OBLA

Critical Power & OBLA
Critical power = athlete's maximal oxidative energy capacity. Rate of lactate production = rate of clearance by muscles + other tissues.
OBLA (Onset of Blood Lactate Accumulation) Occurs at higher % of VO₂max in trained athletes: up to 80–90% of aerobic capacity vs ≈50–60% in untrained.
Why it matters Aerobic training ↑ absolute lactate threshold → athlete can work at a higher relative AND absolute % of VO₂max before lactate accumulates.
Performance Impact
Two athletes with same VO₂max (e.g., 50 mL·kg⁻¹·min⁻¹) can have vastly different performances based on lactate threshold:
Athlete A — 80% threshold Threshold at 40 mL·kg⁻¹·min⁻¹ → maintains 5 mL·kg⁻¹·min⁻¹ higher power output than Athlete B.
Athlete B — 70% threshold Threshold at 35 mL·kg⁻¹·min⁻¹ → slower race pace despite identical VO₂max.
Practical outcomes Running at higher % VO₂max pace · enhanced recovery for second-half performance · higher sustained exercise intensities throughout an event.

In experienced athletes whose VO₂max has plateaued, lactate threshold and exercise economy become the primary performance differentiators.

Topic 01 — CV Adaptations · Exercise Economy

Exercise Economy

What It Is
Exercise economy = energy cost per unit of work or movement at a given speed/power output. Primarily a function of biomechanics and technique, influenced by metabolic, cardiovascular, and neuromuscular efficiency.
Why it matters Two athletes with identical VO₂max and lactate threshold can have different race outcomes. Better economy = faster pace for the same energy expenditure.
What Improves It
Neural adaptations ↑ Motor recruitment efficiency, ↓ muscle activity variability between strides/pedal strokes
Elastic energy storage ↑ Muscle coactivation → ↑ eccentric-to-concentric ratio → ↑ use of stored elastic energy → ↓ metabolic cost
Substrate efficiency ↑ Fat oxidation → glycogen sparing → prolonged high-intensity performance
Technique refinement Less muscle activity per unit of work; shorter durations of muscle activation in trained cyclists and runners
Body fat & economy: Aerobic endurance training ↓ % body fat. For endurance sports where the athlete must move body mass (running), ↓ fat mass = less metabolic cost per stride = ↑ economy. Excessive training without adequate nutrition → catabolic dominance → impaired recovery and performance.

Topic 02

Respiratory
Adaptations

How pulmonary ventilation, tidal volume, and breathing frequency change — and why training specificity limits the transfer of these adaptations

Topic 02 — Respiratory Adaptations

Does the Lung Limit Performance?

General Rule
Pulmonary ventilation generally does not limit aerobic exercise — the respiratory system can accommodate the demands of high-intensity aerobic endurance exercise for the majority of athletes.
Unaffected or moderately affected Aerobic training either leaves the respiratory system unaffected or only moderately changes it.
Exception — elite highly trained athletes In some highly trained aerobic endurance athletes, total body aerobic endurance exercise may exceed the respiratory system's functional capacity and affect O₂ transport to locomotor musculature.
Training Adaptations Include
↑ Tidal volume Volume of air per breath increases with maximal exercise.
↑ Breathing frequency Increases with maximal exercise demand.
Submaximal shift During submaximal activity, breathing frequency is often reduced while tidal volume is increased — each breath carries more air, fewer breaths needed.
Table 6.2 data Maximal pulmonary ventilation: 128.7 → 156.4 L/min (untrained to trained). Elite male: 164.4 L/min.
Ventilatory adaptations result from local, neural, or chemical adaptations in the specific muscles trained through exercise. These adaptations are highly specific to the type of exercise used — not generalisable across all activities.
Topic 02 — Respiratory Adaptations · Specificity

Ventilatory Specificity

Specificity Rule
Ventilatory adaptations observed during lower extremity exercise primarily occur as a result of lower extremity training.
Key implication If training focuses on the lower extremities (e.g., running, cycling), it reduces lactate levels and CO₂ production from lactate buffering in those muscles — but will not produce ventilatory adaptations during upper extremity activities, where no training adaptations have taken place.
Maximal vs Submaximal
Maximal exercise ↑ Tidal volume + ↑ breathing frequency — both increase to meet maximum ventilatory demand.
Submaximal exercise ↓ Breathing frequency + ↑ tidal volume — more efficient gas exchange per breath. This is why trained athletes appear to breathe more slowly at the same absolute workload as untrained individuals.
Resting ventilation Resting: 10.9 → 12.0 L/min (minimal change). Elite male: 11.8 L/min. The adaptation is primarily in the response to exercise, not rest.
!
Practical implication for S&C professionals: A runner who develops excellent respiratory efficiency during running will not automatically show the same ventilatory adaptations during swimming or upper-body ergometry. Sport-specific aerobic conditioning must be developed in the context of the actual movement patterns of the sport.
Topic 02 — Recap

Respiratory Adaptations — Recap

✅
General Rule
Pulmonary ventilation does not limit aerobic exercise for most athletes. Respiratory system can accommodate high-intensity aerobic endurance demands. Exception: some highly trained elite athletes may approach respiratory limits.
✅
Key Adaptations
↑ Tidal volume + ↑ breathing frequency at maximal exercise. Submaximal shift: ↓ breathing frequency + ↑ tidal volume = more efficient gas exchange per breath. Max ventilation: 128.7 → 156.4 L/min (untrained to trained); elite male: 164.4 L/min.
✅
Specificity
Ventilatory adaptations are highly specific to the muscles trained. Lower extremity training adaptations do not transfer to upper extremity ventilatory response. Result from local, neural, or chemical adaptations in the specific trained muscles.

The respiratory system is not the limiting factor in most athletes — but its adaptations are rigorously specific to the mode of training.

Topic 03

Neural
Adaptations

How chronic aerobic training improves movement economy, muscle coactivation patterns, and elastic energy use — even without the neural gains seen in strength training

Topic 03 — Neural Adaptations

Movement Economy & Neuromuscular Efficiency

Understudied but Important
Neural adaptations from chronic aerobic training are relatively understudied compared to strength and power training. Nonetheless, clear adaptations occur — particularly in movement economy.
Key adaptations ↑ Movement economy through improvements in motor recruitment patterns. ↑ Muscle coactivation. ↑ Leg stiffness. ↑ Ratio of eccentric-to-concentric muscle activity.
Net effect Athletes store and use elastic energy more efficiently → ↓ metabolic cost of exercise.
Evidence in Cyclists & Runners
Trained cyclists and runners exhibit less variability in muscle activity between pedal strokes and strides vs novice counterparts.
Shorter muscle activation duration Less muscle activity required per unit of movement — each contraction is more precisely timed and economical.
Triathletes (multisport interference) Triathletes demonstrate more extensive and variable muscle coactivation, with less modulation and refined patterns during cycling and running compared to single-sport athletes — reflecting interference with neuromuscular adaptations from multisport training.
Chronic aerobic training → ↑ Motor recruitment precision → ↑ Eccentric-to-concentric ratio → ↑ Elastic energy use → ↓ Metabolic cost = ↑ Economy
Topic 03 — Neural Adaptations · Elastic Energy

Leg Stiffness & Elastic Energy

Muscle Coactivation ↑
Chronic aerobic training → ↑ muscle coactivation (simultaneous activation of agonist + antagonist muscle groups around a joint).
Functional benefit ↑ Leg stiffness during running → allows the musculotendinous unit to store more elastic potential energy during the eccentric (landing) phase.
Release on concentric phase Stored elastic energy released during the propulsive phase → reduces ATP demand for force production → ↓ metabolic cost per stride.
Eccentric-to-Concentric Ratio
Greater ratio of eccentric-to-concentric muscle activity enables athletes to:
Use stored elastic energy more efficiently The stretch-shortening cycle (SSC) is optimised — longer pre-activation periods and better muscle stiffness regulation.
Reduce metabolic cost Each stride costs less metabolically despite the same mechanical output — this is the neural component of running economy improvement.
Contrast with strength training RT neural adaptations focus on ↑ recruitment and ↑ firing rate. Aerobic neural adaptations focus on pattern refinement and energy recycling.
Multisport interference: Triathletes show more extensive and variable muscle coactivation with less modulation during cycling and running than single-sport athletes — suggesting that training across multiple disciplines creates some interference with the refinement of sport-specific neuromuscular patterns. This has implications for concurrent training programme design.
Topic 03 — Recap

Neural Adaptations — Recap

✅
Movement Economy
Chronic aerobic training → ↑ movement economy via improvements in motor recruitment. Less variability in muscle activity per stride/pedal stroke. Shorter durations of muscle activation in trained athletes.
✅
Elastic Energy & Leg Stiffness
↑ Muscle coactivation → ↑ leg stiffness → ↑ elastic energy storage during eccentric phase → released on concentric phase → ↓ metabolic cost per stride. Greater eccentric-to-concentric ratio is the neural mechanism behind running economy gains.
✅
Multisport Interference
Triathletes show more extensive + variable muscle coactivation with less modulation vs single-sport athletes during cycling and running — neuromuscular pattern refinement is partially disrupted by multisport training demands.

Aerobic neural adaptations are about efficiency and energy recycling — not recruitment and firing rate. The nervous system learns to do more with less.

Topic 04 — Muscular Adaptations · 1 of 4

↑ Mitochondria & Myoglobin

Mitochondria
Aerobic training → ↑ size and number of mitochondria in trained muscle fibers.
What mitochondria do Aerobically produce ATP via oxidation of pyruvate, lactate, and free fatty acids (products of glycolysis). The larger and more numerous the mitochondria, the greater the capacity to extract and use O₂.
Table 6.2 data Succinate dehydrogenase (SDH) activity: 6.4 → 17.7 μmol·g⁻¹·min⁻¹ (nearly 3× increase). Elite: 21.6 μmol·g⁻¹·min⁻¹. Citrate synthase: 35.9 → 45.1 μmol·min⁻¹·g⁻¹.
Mitochondrial density ↑ Unlike resistance training (where CSA increase dilutes density), aerobic training directly ↑ both absolute number and density of mitochondria.
Myoglobin
Myoglobin = protein that transports oxygen within the cell (intracellular O₂ store and shuttle).
Aerobic training → ↑ myoglobin content When combined with ↑ and larger mitochondria, the capacity of muscle tissue to extract and use O₂ is greatly enhanced.
Triple synergy ↑ Capillary density (O₂ delivery) + ↑ myoglobin (O₂ transport within cell) + ↑ mitochondria (O₂ utilisation) = multiplicative improvement in muscle aerobic capacity.
Contrast with RT Resistance training → ↑ CSA but ↓ mitochondrial density (diluted). Aerobic training → no major CSA gain but large absolute increase in mitochondrial content.
Topic 04 — Muscular Adaptations · 2 & 3 of 4

Oxidative Enzymes & Glycogen Sparing

↑ Oxidative Enzyme Activity
Aerobic training → ↑ levels and activity of enzymes involved in oxidative metabolism — the biochemical infrastructure for aerobic energy production.
Citrate synthase Krebs cycle rate-limiting enzyme. 35.9 → 45.1 μmol·min⁻¹·g⁻¹ (Table 6.2).
Succinate dehydrogenase (SDH) Krebs cycle + electron transport chain marker. 6.4 → 17.7 (↑ 177%). Elite: 21.6.
Lactate dehydrogenase Variable response — may decrease (less reliance on glycolysis).
Fat-metabolising enzymes ↑ activity → ↑ ability to oxidise free fatty acids → ↑ fat use during exercise.
Glycogen Sparing & ↑ Fat Oxidation
Aerobic training → greater use of fat as fuel at any given exercise intensity, with relative sparing of carbohydrate (glycogen).
Why it matters With glycogen sparing, an endurance athlete can maintain higher-intensity exercise for longer before glycogen depletion limits performance.
Mechanism ↑ Fat-metabolising enzyme activity + ↑ mitochondrial respiratory capacity → shift in substrate use from carbohydrate toward fat at the same absolute intensity.
Substrate stores ↑ Stored ATP, CP, glycogen, triglycerides (Table 6.1). More fuel available + more efficient use of fat = superior endurance performance.
Also worth noting: Aerobic training can spare muscle glycogen (less glycogen use during exercise) AND increase fat use within the muscle — both attributed to ↑ fat-metabolising enzymes + ↑ mitochondrial respiratory capacity. This helps prolong performance at the same exercise intensity.
Topic 04 — Muscular Adaptations · 4 of 4

Fiber Type Transitions

IIx → IIa Transition
Little evidence that Type II → Type I conversion occurs with aerobic training. However, there may be a gradual conversion within Type II subgroups: IIx → IIa.
Why significant Type IIa fibers have greater oxidative capacity than IIx — more similar functionally to Type I. This shift makes the muscle overall more aerobically efficient.
Table 6.2 data % Type I fibers: 48% → 51% (untrained before → after). Elite male: 60.8%. Elite female: 60.4–61.8%. Aerobic training → ↑ oxidative phenotype of muscle.
Hybrid Fibers & Fiber Size
Hybrid fibers = fibers containing a combination of two or three MHC isoforms (pure + mixed). Can increase or decrease depending on muscle composition + exercise stimulus.
Marathon training study (Trappe et al.) 16-week marathon training (+ 3-week taper): ≈50% ↓ in hybrid fiber %. ↑ Type I fibers in gastrocnemius. Type I volume-adjusted fibers are influenced by training volume.
Fiber size — unexpected finding 13 weeks marathon training → ↓ 20% in fiber size (both Type I and II) — while maintaining strength and actually ↑ Type IIa strength by 18% after 3-week taper. Smaller fibers → shorter O₂ diffusion distance → ↑ aerobic efficiency.
Relative increases in aerobic potential are similar in Type I and Type II fibers with endurance training — but Type I fibers have a higher pre-existing initial aerobic capacity, so their absolute aerobic capacity remains greater both before and after training.

Topic 04 — Muscular Adaptations · Body Composition

Body Composition & Protein Turnover

Body Composition
Aerobic endurance training is generally associated with ↓ relative % body fat — assuming appropriate nutritional intake.
Fat-free mass Little or no significant effect on fat-free mass (Table 6.1). Contrast with resistance training which ↑ fat-free mass.
Longer programs Greater decreases in % body fat with longer-term programs.
Caution Excessive training → catabolic dominance → imbalance between catabolic + anabolic processes → impaired performance and health.
Protein Turnover
Aerobic training, especially running, often associated with ↑ net protein breakdown from muscle acutely — driven by acute cortisol secretion.
Despite anabolic hormones Even with concomitant ↑ testosterone and IGF-1, net protein synthesis in skeletal muscle of aerobic endurance athletes does occur — likely due to mitochondrial rather than contractile proteins.
Recovery nutrition Post-exercise nutrition incorporating high-quality proteins + carbohydrates is critical — mitochondrial and myofibrillar protein turnover rates differ, and protein ingestion timing influences which pathway is prioritised.
RED-S (Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport): Low energy availability can affect the degree of protein involvement in aerobic endurance exercise, particularly during long-duration bouts. Consideration must be given to how training variables are manipulated and energy supplies are managed across a season. See Chapter 11 for full detail.

How aerobic exercise modality determines skeletal adaptation — and what the evidence says about running and joint health

Topic 05 — Bone & Connective Tissue

Bone Adaptation is Activity-Specific

Running — High Impact
Running promotes bone growth more effectively than most other aerobic exercises — due to repetitive high-impact loading in which the body experiences forces several times its own weight.
Runners vs controls Runners had greater total and leg bone mineral density compared to non-exercising controls.
Runners vs cyclists Runners also had greater total + leg BMD than cyclists (who had reduced lower spine BMD compared to non-exercising controls).
Cycling & Swimming — Weight-Supported
Cycling and swimming are weight-supported activities that place minimal strain on the skeletal system → less stimulation for bone growth.
Cyclists — reduced spine BMD Cyclists had reduced lower spine BMD compared to non-exercising controls — non-weight-bearing aerobic exercise can actually reduce bone density in areas not used for locomotion.
Practical implication Cyclists and swimmers should include weight-bearing activities (e.g., resistance training or running) to counteract the potential reductions in bone mass associated with non-weight-bearing activities.
Key principle: Skeletal adaptations to aerobic endurance training are specific to the activity undertaken — more specifically, the axial loading that the activity induces. The bone responds to the magnitude and rate of the mechanical load, not just the aerobic demand.
Topic 05 — Bone & Connective Tissue · Bone Growth Principles

Stimulating Bone Growth via Aerobic Exercise

1
Exceed MES and strain frequency: Activity must be significantly more intense than daily activities to exceed the minimum threshold intensity AND the cyclical strain frequency needed for bone growth. Progressive overload applies to bone as to muscle.
2
↑ Rate of limb movement: Bone responds to both the magnitude AND rate of external loading. High-intensity interval training techniques provide a greater osteogenic stimulus while still delivering aerobic training benefits.
3
Concurrent training for bone: Combining aerobic endurance training with resistance training is a known stimulus for improving bone mass — the strongest combination for osteogenic effect. RT provides the high mechanical loads that aerobic training often cannot.
4
Oxygen transport limitation: Eventually, further overloading bone through aerobic exercise becomes difficult because the oxygen transport system (not musculoskeletal limitations) restricts progression to new exercise intensities. This is when RT must supplement aerobic training for bone benefit.
High-impact cyclical loading: Gymnastics, volleyball, basketball → selectively ↑ BMD at hip + spine. These activities expose bone to repeated high forces — more osteogenic than low-impact continuous activities even at the same aerobic intensity.
Topic 05 — Bone & Connective Tissue · Soft Tissues

Tendons, Ligaments & Cartilage

Tendons & Ligaments
In mature adults, tendon and ligament growth and strength are proportional to the exercise stimulus intensity, especially from weight-bearing activities.
Structural adaptation Collagen-containing fibers ↑ in number, size, and density with consistent exercise stimulation.
Threshold requirement Exercise intensity must consistently exceed the strain placed on connective tissues during normal daily activities to create changes. Low-intensity aerobic exercise may be insufficient.
Table 6.1 Ligament strength: ↑. Tendon strength: ↑. Collagen content: Variable.
Cartilage
Weight-bearing activity has a positive effect on cartilage — demonstrated in the knee joint, where weight-bearing surfaces are thicker than non-weight-bearing surfaces.
Complete ROM essential Complete movement through a full ROM during weight bearing is likely essential to maintain tissue viability — ensures synovial fluid circulation and nutrient delivery.
Running & knee OA debate A study of thousands of non-elite runners (ages 12–50) reported that running did not increase odds of knee pain or symptomatic knee OA. Running may even offer protection via improved periarticular strength + proprioception.
Elite aerobic athletes & OA: Former athletes from power + mixed-sports backgrounds (soccer, basketball, track) had increased rates of premature OA admissions vs aerobic endurance athletes (long-distance runners) — who experienced admissions but at a notably older age. Lower BMI + active lifestyle may provide protection.
Topic 05 — Bone & Connective Tissue · Running & Joints

Running & Knee Osteoarthritis — The Evidence

Concern (Historic View)
Habitual running suggested to have a negative impact on joints like the knees and hips — increasing risk of cartilage damage and osteoarthritis due to repetitive impact loading.
Current Evidence
Running does not increase the odds of knee pain or symptomatic knee OA in non-elite runners. Running does not increase risk of symptomatic knee OA vs non-running activities.
Possible Protection
Some research suggests running may even offer protection against general knee pain — associated with improved periarticular strength + proprioception that comes with the running motion.
Elite Athlete Caveat
Level of exposure (duration, intensity, frequency) may be a risk factor for knee OA in elite athletes. Power/mixed-sport athletes show earlier onset OA than aerobic endurance athletes.
Protective mechanisms in aerobic endurance athletes: Lower body mass index assists in preventing OA. More physically active lifestyle → higher-than-average life expectancy → later onset of OA if it does occur. Findings did not show notable differences between elite and non-elite running athletes.

Endocrine
Adaptations

How hormonal responses to aerobic exercise vary with intensity and duration — and why energy availability and recovery nutrition are critical endocrine regulators

Topic 06 — Endocrine Adaptations

Hormonal Response — Maximal vs Submaximal

Maximal Exercise
High-intensity aerobic training augments absolute secretion rates of many hormones in response to maximal exercise.
Why Greater hormonal response patterns at maximal exercise appear to augment the athlete's ability to tolerate and sustain prolonged high-intensity exercise.
Fight-or-flight (very short, very high) When exercise intensity is very high and duration very short (5–10 s), only fight-or-flight changes in peripheral blood hormone concentrations occur: ↑ epinephrine + norepinephrine.
Submaximal Exercise
Trained athletes have attenuated hormonal responses to submaximal exercise — the same absolute workload produces a smaller hormonal reaction in the trained athlete.
But same relative intensity The hormonal response of trained athletes is similar to untrained persons when performing aerobic exercise at the same relative submaximal exercise intensity (% of VO₂max).
Practical implication A trained athlete exercising at 70% VO₂max shows similar hormonal response to an untrained person at 70% VO₂max — but the trained athlete can sustain a much higher absolute workload at that relative intensity.
Key to endocrine responses in aerobic athletes: The interaction between training variables (volume + intensity + duration of activity) and its influence on energy supply. These factors collectively determine both the endocrine response and the adaptations experienced during and after training.
Topic 06 — Endocrine Adaptations · Key Hormones

Testosterone, Cortisol & GH / IGF-1

Testosterone & Insulin
Over the course of a season, high-level male aerobic endurance runners' basal testosterone + insulin concentrations may change and become more sensitive to variations in training parameters (intensity + distance run each week) — unlike cortisol which is less sensitive to these variations.

Athletes who ran greater mileage with low energy availability → ↓ basal testosterone.
Cortisol
Aerobic training, especially running → ↑ net protein breakdown from muscle acutely — partly driven by acute stress-induced cortisol secretion.

Research on elite rowers: testosterone + cortisol more sensitive to changes in training volume than growth hormone. Cortisol less sensitive to weekly intensity + distance variations than testosterone.
Growth Hormone & IGF-1
GH, IGF-1, testosterone, and insulin affect the integrity of muscle, bone, and connective tissue + assist in maintaining metabolism within a normal range.

Evidence suggests net protein synthesis in skeletal muscle of aerobic endurance athletes does occur — likely due to mitochondrial rather than contractile proteins (unlike RT where contractile protein synthesis dominates).
Epinephrine & Norepinephrine
Very high intensity + very short duration (5–10 s) → primarily catecholamine (fight-or-flight) response — ↑ epinephrine + norepinephrine.

These mediate acute cardiovascular, metabolic, and neuromuscular responses to explosive aerobic exercise demands.
Topic 06 — Endocrine Adaptations · Energy Availability

Energy Availability & RED-S

Low Energy Availability Effects
Low energy availability can affect the degree of protein involvement in aerobic endurance exercise, particularly during long-duration bouts.
Testosterone ↓ Athletes who ran greater mileage with low energy availability showed ↓ basal testosterone concentrations.
Hormonal sensitivity Testosterone + insulin concentrations are more sensitive to energy availability and training load changes than cortisol or GH — making them useful monitoring markers.
Protein catabolism ↑ Low energy → greater reliance on protein as fuel → accelerated muscle breakdown + impaired adaptation.
RED-S (Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport)
RED-S = impaired physiological function caused by relative energy deficiency — affects endocrine, metabolic, haematological, immunological, psychological, and bone health systems.
Athletes at risk Endurance athletes with high training volumes + inadequate energy intake are at particular risk. Was previously known as the Female Athlete Triad but now recognised to affect all athletes regardless of sex.
Endocrine impact ↓ Testosterone · ↓ estrogen · ↓ IGF-1 · ↑ cortisol → bone loss, impaired muscle adaptation, hormonal suppression, and impaired recovery.
See Chapter 11 For full detail on RED-S management and nutritional strategies.
Recovery nutrition is critical: Post-exercise nutrition incorporating high-quality proteins + carbohydrates is essential for aerobic endurance athletes. Mitochondrial and myofibrillar protein turnover rates differ — protein ingestion timing may give preference to one pathway over another. Early post-exercise nutrition is especially important during high-volume training periods.
Topic 06 — Endocrine Adaptations · Protein Pathways

Protein Pathways in Aerobic Athletes

1
Acute protein breakdown: Aerobic exercise (especially running) → acute ↑ in net protein breakdown from muscle — driven by acute stress-induced cortisol secretion despite concomitant ↑ in anabolic hormones (testosterone, IGF-1).
2
Mitochondrial vs contractile protein synthesis: Net protein synthesis in skeletal muscle of aerobic athletes does occur, but primarily mitochondrial proteins rather than contractile proteins (actin/myosin) — contrast with resistance training where contractile protein synthesis dominates.
3
Differential turnover rates: Mitochondrial and myofibrillar protein turnover rates may differ — influencing muscle recovery capacity post-exercise. Protein ingestion may alter which pathway (mitochondrial vs myofibrillar) is prioritised during recovery.
4
Session intensity interacts with recovery: High-intensity vs low-intensity sessions may alter protein pathway preference. Exercise-nutrient interactions at different intensities are still evolving as a research area — but early post-exercise protein + carbohydrate remains the consistent recommendation.
5
Muscle hypertrophy in aerobic athletes: Possible — most likely from mitochondrial protein accumulation, not myofibrillar addition. This explains why aerobic endurance athletes can have well-developed oxidative capacity without the CSA gains seen in resistance-trained athletes.
Aerobic exercise → ↑ Cortisol (acute) → ↑ Protein breakdown + ↑ Anabolic hormones → Net: ↑ mitochondrial protein synthesis

Aerobic vs Anaerobic — Endocrine Comparison

Aerobic Training
• ↑ Absolute secretion at maximal exercise
• ↓ Response at same absolute submaximal workload
• Same response at same relative submaximal intensity
• Primarily mitochondrial protein synthesis
• Testosterone sensitive to volume + energy availability
• Cortisol sensitive to training volume (rowers)
• RED-S risk with high volume + low energy availability
Anaerobic/Resistance Training
• Acute spike at each session (testosterone, GH)
• Resting concentrations: unchanged chronically
• AR upregulated 48–72 hrs post-workout
• Primarily contractile protein synthesis (actin/myosin)
• ↑ mTOR pathway dominates
• High-volume protocols → transient AR downregulation
• Peri-workout nutrition attenuates AR downregulation
Shared principle: In both aerobic and anaerobic training, the acute hormonal response during and immediately after exercise is the primary driver of tissue adaptation — not chronic basal hormone elevation. Recovery nutrition + training load management are the key modulators.

Topic 07 — External Factors · 1 of 6

Altitude — Acute & Chronic Responses

Acute Responses (≥3,900 ft / 1,189 m)
Immediate adjustments to compensate for ↓ partial pressure of O₂:
① Hyperventilation ↑ breathing frequency (primarily); ventilation stabilises with time
② ↑ Submaximal cardiac output due primarily to ↑ HR (30–50% above sea-level values)
③ Stroke volume remains constant or slightly ↓
④ ↓ Arterial O₂ saturation → ↓ VO₂max + ↓ aerobic performance
⑤ Body fluids become more alkaline due to ↓ CO₂ with hyperventilation
Chronic Acclimatization
Within 10–14 days: HR + cardiac output return toward normal values due to longer-term acclimatization response — ↑ red blood cell production (polycythemia):
↑ Hemoglobin (5–15%; up to 30–50% RBC ↑)
↑ Diffusing capacity of O₂ through pulmonary membranes
↑ Capillarisation + ↑ mitochondria in skeletal muscle
↑ Fat use, sparing glycogen
Acid-base balance maintenance via renal HCO₃⁻ excretion + hyperventilation
Minimum 3–6 weeks needed to adapt at moderate altitude (2,200–3,000 ft / 608–914 m)
Altitude mechanism chain: ↓ Ambient O₂ → ↓ arterial O₂ content → ↑ HIF-α stabilisation → ↑ EPO → ↑ Hb mass + RBC volume → ↑ a-vO₂ differential → ↑ VO₂max → ↑ aerobic endurance performance. Acclimatization changes revert approximately one month after return to sea level.
Topic 07 — External Factors · 2 of 6

Heat Acclimatization

Key Chronic Adaptations
Repeated exposure to hot environment → thermoregulatory + physiological adaptations:
Earlier onset of sweating + ↑ sweat rate + ↑ evaporative cooling
↓ Resting + exercising skin + core body temperature
↑ Plasma volume (sudden, large expansion) → ↑ blood volume → ↑ SV → ↑ VO₂max
↓ Resting + exercising HR
↑ Myocardial protection + ↑ heat shock proteins
↓ Gastrointestinal distress
Timeline & Decay
Adaptations start from the first day of exposure. ~75% of adaptations reported to occur within first 4–6 days.
Decay rate For every day without heat exposure: 2.5% loss of adaptations. Full loss expected after ~1 month without repeated heat stress.
Aerobic fitness advantage Higher aerobic fitness → greater ability to exercise in heat + better retention of heat acclimatization adaptations.
Sex differences Females experience greater ↑ in body temperature in heat (lower thermosensitivity + lower total sweat rate) → may require slightly longer protocol to establish thermoregulatory stability.
Heat mechanism chain: ↑ Core body temperature → ↑ heat shock proteins → ↑ HIF-α stabilisation → ↑ aldosterone → ↑ plasma volume → ↑ SV + → ↑ VO₂max + ↑ lactate threshold → ↑ aerobic endurance performance. Also: ↑ VEGF → ↑ skeletal muscle blood flow.
Topic 07 — External Factors · Altitude vs Heat

Altitude vs Heat — Which Protocol?

Altitude Training
Mechanism: Hypobaric-hypoxic environment → ↓ arterial O₂ → ↑ HIF-α → ↑ EPO → ↑ Hb mass + RBC → ↑ a-vO₂ diff → ↑ VO₂max

↓ Plasma volume acutely (contrast with heat acclimatization which ↑ plasma volume)

Longer exposure times required than heat. Best when competing at altitude.
Heat Acclimatization
Mechanism: ↑ Core temp → ↑ aldosterone → ↑ plasma volume → ↑ SV → ↑ VO₂max + ↑ lactate threshold

Less expensive + easier access than altitude. Thermoregulatory benefits + performance in thermoneutral + hot environments. Adaptations decay 2.5%/day without re-exposure.
Combining Both
Theoretically offers the best of both (erythropoiesis + plasma expansion). However, altitude acutely ↓ plasma volume — opposing heat acclimatization's ↑ plasma volume effect. Combined group showed limited additional physiological benefit vs heat-only group in exercise in hot environments.
Decision Rule
Match protocol to competition environment.
Race at altitude (2,500–3,000 m) → altitude training.
Race at sea level in hot/humid conditions → heat acclimatization.
Non-hematological adaptations are specific to training environment. Individualise — not all athletes respond the same way.
Topic 07 — External Factors · 3 & 4 of 6

Hyperoxic Breathing · Smoking & Vaping

Hyperoxic Breathing
Breathing O₂-enriched gas mixtures during rest or following exercise → short-term improvements:
↑ O₂ transport · ↑ Lactate metabolism · ↑ Power output · ↑ Work tolerance vs normoxic breathing
Long-term supplementation Systematic supplementation during endurance training over time shows contradictory results and overall small benefits. Normal sea-level breathing results in 95–98% arterial Hb O₂ saturation — already near maximum. The potential for hyperoxic breathing remains to be fully elucidated.
Smoking & Vaping
Both impair aerobic exercise performance:
Smoking — key mechanisms ① ↑ Airway resistance (bronchiole constriction). ② Paralysis of cilia → debris accumulation. ③ Carboxyhemoglobin (CO binds Hb > O₂) → ↓ O₂ carrying capacity → ↑ submaximal CV responses.
Vaping Most common nicotine modality in young people (27.5% US high school students in 2019). Regular vaping → ↓ VO₂peak. Short-term: ↑ HR + diastolic BP via catecholamine release. Lung inflammation + cellular damage → ↓ lung capacity + impaired gas exchange. Long-term CV changes (heart failure risk).
Vaping vs smoking: Vaping users show significantly slower 2-mile run times than non-vapers — even slower than traditional cigarette smokers in some studies. Lower O₂ extraction, reduced chronotropic responses, and ↓ VO₂peak even in young, healthy adults who vape regularly.
Topic 07 — External Factors · 5 & 6 of 6

Blood Doping & Genetic Potential

Blood Doping
Artificially ↑ red blood cell mass via:
RBC infusion (own or donor) — rapid ↑ for a few weeks
EPO administration — slower ↑ but sustained as long as EPO is given
Both → ↑ O₂ carrying capacity → ↑ O₂ to working muscles → ↑ VO₂max up to 11%. At submaximal workloads: ↓ HR, ↓ blood lactate, ↑ pH.
Health risks ↑ Hematocrit → ↑ blood viscosity → risk of stroke, MI, DVT, pulmonary embolism. ↑ Arterial BP. Flu-like symptoms. Risks from transfusion/infusion.
Genetic Potential
The upper limit of individual genetic potential contributes significantly to the absolute magnitude of training adaptations.
System-level ceiling Each biological system (e.g., cardiovascular) has an upper adaptation limit. As athletes approach this limit, smaller and smaller gains are observed per unit of training.
Elite performance implications In elite competitions (e.g., swimming), small gains may be the difference between gold and 26th place. In an event where tenths or hundredths of a second matter, a 0.05% performance improvement may be worth significant training investment.
Program design + monitoring In elite athletes near their genetic ceiling, careful program design and monitoring become even more critical to extract the small remaining gains.
Blood doping + altitude interaction: As altitude increases, the positive influence of blood doping is reduced — bone marrow is already stimulated maximally by hypoxia. Blood doping benefits primarily those already acclimatized to heat, providing very little aid to non-acclimatized individuals during heat exposure.

Topic 08

Detraining

The partial or complete loss of aerobic training adaptations in response to an insufficient training stimulus — and how it differs from tapering

Topic 08 — Detraining

Detraining vs Tapering

Detraining
Detraining = partial or complete loss of training-induced adaptations in response to an insufficient training stimulus.
Governed by Principle of training reversibility — training-induced adaptations are transient and disappear when training load is insufficient or removed.
Results in Partial or complete reversal of adaptations → compromised athletic performance. Aerobic endurance adaptations most sensitive to inactivity because of their enzymatic basis.
Prevention Proper exercise variation, intensity, maintenance programs, and active recovery periods can adequately protect against serious detraining effects.
Tapering (NOT Detraining)
Tapering = planned reduction in training volume (duration + frequency but not intensity) before competition or planned recovery microcycle.
Purpose Designed to enhance athletic performance + adaptations — allows accumulated fatigue to dissipate while maintaining fitness.
Key distinction Intensity is maintained during tapering → neuromuscular + metabolic adaptations are preserved. Volume ↓ allows residual fatigue to clear → often results in performance peaks.
Marathon study example After 13 weeks marathon training (↓ fiber size 20%), a 3-week taper → ↑ Type IIa fiber strength by 18% — demonstrating the performance benefit of planned volume reduction.
Topic 08 — Detraining · VO₂max Loss

VO₂max Loss — Timeline

Short-Term
< 4 weeks
↓ 4–14% VO₂max (Highly Trained Athletes)
Reduction primarily from ↓ blood volume → ↓ stroke volume → ↓ max cardiac output. ↑ Submaximal HR. These cardiovascular changes are the primary early driver of VO₂max loss — not enzymatic changes.
Long-Term
> 4 weeks
↓ 6–20% VO₂max (Highly Trained Athletes)
Reduction from ↓ blood volume, ↓ stroke volume, ↓ max cardiac output AND ↑ submaximal HR. Enzymatic changes now also contribute — loss of oxidative enzyme activity and mitochondrial density. The loss of aerobic endurance performance supports the loss of VO₂max.
Mechanism
of Loss
Why VO₂max Drops — The Cascade
↓ Blood volume → ↓ Stroke volume → ↓ Max cardiac output + ↑ Submaximal HR → ↓ VO₂max → ↓ Aerobic endurance performance. The exact cellular mechanisms dictating detraining changes are still unknown.
Key references: Two review articles address factors contributing to aerobic performance — most importantly VO₂max (cardiac output + a-vO₂ difference) — and the impact of short-term (<4 weeks) and long-term (>4 weeks) detraining. Highly trained athletes: short-term ↓ 4–14%; long-term ↓ 6–20%.
Topic 08 — Detraining · Enzymatic Basis

Enzymatic Sensitivity & Prevention

Why Aerobic Adaptations Are Most Sensitive
Aerobic endurance adaptations are most sensitive to periods of inactivity because of their enzymatic basis — oxidative enzymes rapidly lose activity without sustained training stimulus.
Exact mechanisms unknown The cellular mechanisms that dictate detraining changes are unknown; further research needed. What is known: enzyme activity and mitochondrial density are highly labile.
Contrast with resistance training Strength detraining is slower and less pronounced — structural changes (CSA) persist longer than the biochemical infrastructure of aerobic fitness.
Prevention Strategies
Exercise variation Varying training modalities maintains stimulus across different muscle groups + metabolic pathways — reduces risk of staleness + provides continued stimulus for enzymatic maintenance.
Maintain intensity During reduced training (e.g., tapering, travel, off-season), maintaining exercise intensity while reducing volume preserves most aerobic adaptations for significantly longer than reducing intensity.
Active recovery periods Even low-intensity activity during planned recovery periods maintains minimum enzymatic activity and cardiovascular adaptations.
Maintenance programs Reduced-frequency maintenance programs (even 1–2×/week high-intensity sessions) can preserve VO₂max adaptations for extended periods.

Proper exercise variation, intensity maintenance, and active recovery can adequately protect against serious detraining effects.

Aerobic Training — The Big Picture

Central Principle
Aerobic endurance exercise produces many acute cardiovascular + respiratory responses — and chronic aerobic endurance training produces many lasting adaptations. These are specific to the stimulus, the training programme, and the individual athlete's genetic potential and nutritional status.
For the S&C Professional
Knowledge of cardiovascular, respiratory, nervous, muscular, bone and connective tissue, and endocrine system responses to aerobic endurance training provides the scientific basis for aerobic conditioning and helps predict the adaptations to expect and monitor during training. Optimal adaptations reflect careful design, implementation, and performance monitoring of strength and conditioning programs.

Every aerobic adaptation — from SA node discharge rate to mitochondrial enzyme activity — serves one master goal: deliver more oxygen to working muscles and use it more efficiently.
