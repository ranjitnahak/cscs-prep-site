Task 02 of 04
Derivation — From F = ma to the Impulse–Momentum Relationship

Show step-by-step how Newton's Second Law (Equation 2.1) leads to the impulse–momentum relationship (Equation 2.2). Present each step clearly with a brief explanation in your own words of what is happening at that step.

1
F = m × a — Start with Newton's 2nd Law. Write out what F, m, and a represent.
2
a = Δv / Δt — Define acceleration as the rate of change of velocity. Explain what Δv and Δt mean in the context of an athlete moving.
3
F = m × (Δv / Δt) — Substitute the definition of acceleration into Newton's 2nd Law.
4
F × Δt = m × Δv — Rearrange by multiplying both sides by Δt. Name the left side (impulse) and the right side (change in momentum). State the units of each.
✓
Conclude: Explain in 2–3 sentences why this relationship is important for strength and conditioning — specifically how it justifies the use of explosive training methods for improving jump performance.
Reference: Ch. 2 — Equations 2.1 & 2.2

Task 04 of 04
Draw & Explain Figure 2.5 — Mechanical Advantage at the Knee

Study Figure 2.5 from your textbook (NSCA CSCS 5th Edition, p. 30), then complete the following:

  • Draw both diagrams (a) and (b) by hand — label the femur, quadriceps tendon, patella, patellar ligament, tibia, axis of rotation, and the moment arm in each diagram.
  • Explain in writing what is happening mechanically in diagram (a): how the patella increases the mechanical advantage of the quadriceps muscle group.
  • Explain diagram (b): what happens to the moment arm and mechanical advantage when the patella is absent, and why this matters clinically.
  • Connect to the equation: Mechanical advantage = muscle moment arm ÷ resistive moment arm. When the patella is absent, the muscle moment arm shortens. Using this relationship, explain how that shortening changes the mechanical advantage value, and what compensatory increase in muscle force would be required to produce the same torque at the knee.
Reference: Figure 2.5 — NSCA CSCS 5th Ed., Ch. 2
